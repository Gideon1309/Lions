Facts about Lions

Introduction

Lions are part of the big cat family(scientific name is pantera leo).
They are second biggest cat in the world. Nearly all wild lions live in Africa,
but one small population exists in Asia.Male lions weigh over 190 kg and can live up to 10 years in the wild,
while females weigh up to 130 kg live up to 16 years.
They can run up to 74 km/h.

What do they eat?

Lions are carnivores meaning they eat meat; they hunt herbivores like hares,
wild hogs, antelopes, zebras, wildebeest, buffaloes, baby elephants, rhinoceros,
hippopotamuses, giraffes and sometimes hunt crocodiles.They can eat up to 40 kg of meat in a single meal.

Lion cubs

After a four-month gestation period, a lioness will sneak away from the pride to give birth to a litter of two to six cubs.
The cubs are about 3 pounds at birth with a yellowish-brown coat and distinct spots or stripes.Cubs remain hidden from the pride
for the first four to six weeks while they gain strength.Lion cubs are quick developers and start to walk at 10 to 15 days old. They also grow baby teeth while they
are very young. Baby lions are totally dependent on their mothers for food and protection during
the first few months. The lionesses will keep her cubs hidden from other lions for up to two months.
A male lion generally stays with his mother for about two years. The mother then runs him out of the group,
and he will be on his own. If the cub is a female, the mother also cares for her until
she is about two years old. Females usually stay with the pride they were born into.

Population

Categorized as a vulnerable population, the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) estimates
somewhere between 23,000 and 39,000 lions remain in the wild.But Tanzania has the most lion with a population of 14,500 lions.

What kills lions?

Lions don,t have natural preditors, but they are usually killed by humans, elephants, rhinoceros, hippopotamuses, crocodiles and hyenas.

what is the main threat to the extinction of lions?

The main threats to African lions are human-wildlife conflict and natural prey decline, as well as habitat loss,
climate change and wildlife trade. With around 23,000 African lions left in the wild, they're now officially
classified as 'vulnerable'.
